## Video script (60–90 seconds, talking-head or slides)

**Hook (0:00–0:10)**
"Quick test: what's the difference between earthing and bonding? If you had to think about it, you're not alone — and in a data center, that difference matters more than most people realize."

**Body (0:10–1:10)**
"Earthing connects the electrical system to the general mass of earth — mainly for safety, so a fault has a clear path back to source and trips a breaker fast.

Bonding is different: it connects metal parts — racks, cable trays, structural steel — to each other so they all sit at the same electrical potential, whether or not that potential is earth.

Here's why data centers care more than a typical office: sensitive electronics are much more affected by tiny potential differences than lighting or general power loads are. If two pieces of equipment are grounded at slightly different potentials and connected by signal cabling, you get a ground loop — stray current flowing where it shouldn't, causing noise or even equipment damage.

That's a bonding problem, not an earthing problem — and it's exactly the kind of intermittent fault that's miserable to diagnose after the fact."

Quick question: what's the actual difference between earthing and bonding?

Earthing = connects the system to earth, mainly for safety (fast fault clearing).
Bonding = connects metal parts to EACH OTHER so they sit at the same potential — whether or not that's earth.

Data centers care about this distinction more than most buildings because sensitive electronics are far more affected by small potential differences than general power loads are. Get bonding wrong and you can create a ground loop — stray current through signal cabling that causes noise or equipment issues, and is miserable to diagnose after the fact.
