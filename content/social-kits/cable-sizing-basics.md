## Video script (60–90 seconds, talking-head or slides)

**Hook (0:00–0:10)**
"Most people think cable sizing is just 'pick a wire big enough for the current.' In a data center, that's wrong — and it's wrong in a way that can actually cause a fire or an outage."

**Body (0:10–1:10)**
"Every cable in a real design has to pass FOUR separate checks, not one:
1. Current rating — can it carry the load current continuously.
2. Derating — does it still carry that current once you account for heat and other cables bundled next to it.
3. Voltage drop — on a long run, does the voltage at the far end stay within limits.
4. Short-circuit withstand — can it survive a fault for as long as it takes the breaker to trip.

Here's the part that surprises people: the current rating almost never governs the final size. On a long run, it's usually voltage drop. Near a busbar or panel, it's usually short-circuit withstand. If you only check current rating, you can end up with a cable that's technically 'big enough' for the load but fails under fault conditions or drops too much voltage at the far end."

## LinkedIn post draft

---
Most engineers learn cable sizing as "pick a wire big enough for the current." That's step one of four, not the whole answer.

A properly sized cable in a data center design has to clear:
→ Current rating (ampacity)
→ Derating for real installation conditions (heat, bundling)
→ Voltage drop over the run
→ Short-circuit withstand

The check that actually governs often isn't the one people expect — long runs are frequently limited by voltage drop, not current. Cables near high fault-current points are frequently limited by short-circuit withstand, not ampacity.
